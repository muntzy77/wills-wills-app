Estate Planning Essentials

Estate planning is more than just drafting a will—it’s a complete strategy to ensure that your assets are managed and distributed according to your wishes and that your loved ones are cared for when you are no longer around. By documenting your intentions clearly, you help minimise delays and disputes and provide peace of mind for those you leave behind.

Why Plan Your Estate?

A comprehensive estate plan allows you to outline who receives your assets, how and when they should receive them, and who will administer your affairs. It can also reduce potential tax liabilities, protect beneficiaries from personal legal issues and ensure that there is a clear roadmap for dealing with your superannuation and insurance benefits.

Key Steps in Estate Planning

  • Initial consultation: Meet with a qualified adviser to discuss your personal circumstances, objectives and concerns. This conversation helps outline the best approach for your estate.
  • Developing a strategy: After gathering information about your assets, liabilities and family situation, an estate planning strategy is prepared. This may include recommendations on wills, trusts and other instruments tailored to your needs.
  • Document preparation and signing: Once you are comfortable with the proposed plan, legal documents are drafted for your review. Your adviser will arrange for any required witnesses, ensure that everything is properly signed and help you store the originals securely.

Understanding Testamentary Trusts

A testamentary trust is a trust created by your will that only comes into effect after your death. Instead of giving assets directly to beneficiaries, you appoint a trustee to hold and manage the assets on their behalf. This structure can provide several advantages, including:

  • Asset protection: Assets held in the trust are generally insulated from beneficiaries’ personal legal issues such as divorce or bankruptcy.
  • Tax flexibility: Trustees can distribute income among beneficiaries in a way that may reduce the overall tax payable, and minors can often receive income tax-free up to certain thresholds.
  • Control and guidance: You can nominate who controls the trust and set out how and when beneficiaries should benefit, which may be especially important if the inheritance is substantial or the beneficiaries are young or vulnerable.

Common Questions

  • Do I need a will? If you own property, have superannuation or any other assets, it is wise to have a will. Without one, state laws determine who inherits your estate, which may not align with your wishes.
  • When should I update my will? Major life events such as marriage, divorce, the birth of a child, buying a home or retiring are good times to review your will and ensure it still reflects your intentions.
  • Who should be my executor? An executor administers your estate. Choose someone you trust to manage your affairs fairly and diligently, and consider nominating an alternative in case your first choice cannot act.
  • Can I appoint guardians? If you have minor children, your will should nominate a guardian to care for them if you and their other parent die. This gives you peace of mind that someone you trust will be responsible for their welfare.
  • What about superannuation and insurance? Superannuation and life insurance benefits often don’t automatically form part of your estate. Consider completing binding nominations to direct these benefits either to your estate or directly to individuals.
